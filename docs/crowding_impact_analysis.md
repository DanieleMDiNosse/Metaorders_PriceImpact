# Crowding and the Proprietary Impact Premium

This note summarizes the recent analyses aimed at answering the question behind
Section 5.4 of the paper:

> Can the larger impact of proprietary metaorders be explained by crowding?

The current answer is nuanced. Crowding is clearly related to impact, and active
directional overlap weakens the proprietary impact premium. The amount explained
depends on the econometric lens: the metaorder-level OLS specification leaves a
positive residual proprietary coefficient, while the power-law-style binned
log-WLS specification makes the residual coefficient smaller and statistically
indistinguishable from zero. In both cases, the evidence does **not** support the
simple mechanical explanation that proprietary metaorders impact more because
they are more numerous and therefore more often overlapped by other metaorders.

The more defensible interpretation is:

> Proprietary metaorders do not face systematically larger time-weighted overlap
> than client metaorders. What differs is the direction of the overlapping flow:
> proprietary metaorders are more often executed while the active surrounding flow
> is aligned with their own direction. This active directional crowding is the
> strongest crowding channel we have found, although its estimated explanatory
> power is stronger in binned log-WLS than in metaorder-level OLS.

## Conceptual Map

The logic of the evidence is now:

1. **Impact premium.** Proprietary metaorders have larger impact on average over
   the empirically relevant range of relative sizes, and their impact is more
   persistent after completion.
2. **Daily crowding.** On a same-day same-ISIN horizon, client metaorders are
   more crowded than proprietary metaorders: client directions are more strongly
   aligned with the leave-one-out imbalance generated by other client
   metaorders, and also more aligned with the all-vs-all same-day imbalance.
3. **Daily magnitude controls.** When impact is regressed at cell level on
   participation rate, duration in volume time, the magnitude of the daily
   imbalance, and a proprietary dummy, the proprietary dummy remains positive.
   This means that the proprietary impact premium is not absorbed by the
   magnitude of the same-day imbalance.
4. **Active interval overlap.** We then refine the crowding notion from
   "alignment with the daily imbalance" to "what other metaorders are actually
   alive during the target metaorder's execution interval." Here the evidence is
   more supportive of a partial crowding channel, but the relevant statistic is
   not simply the number of overlapped metaorders. The key variable is
   directional active overlap: proprietary targets are more often overlapped by
   flow aligned with their own sign.
5. **Power-law-style WLS check.** Because the original proprietary premium was
   observed from log-binned WLS impact curves, the active-overlap regression was
   also repeated on the same `group x Q/V-bin` cells, with `log(mean Impact)` as
   the dependent variable and SEM-based WLS weights. In that specification, the
   proprietary coefficient remains positive but becomes statistically
   indistinguishable from zero after adding active-overlap controls.

So the answer is not "clients crowd more, therefore clients should impact more."
Daily crowding and active interval crowding measure different objects. The first
describes broad same-day order-flow alignment. The second describes the
instantaneous execution environment faced by the target metaorder.

## Definitions

The scalar impact is the standard volatility-normalized signed impact:

```text
Impact_i = Direction_i * PriceChange_i / DailyVol_i
```

The original daily crowding analyses use a leave-one-out imbalance in a
stock-day environment:

```text
c_i = Direction_i * imbalance_i
```

where positive `c_i` means that the target metaorder trades in the same direction
as the surrounding volume-weighted imbalance.

The newer active-overlap analysis replaces stock-day aggregate crowding with a
true interval-based exposure. For target metaorder `i` and another metaorder `j`
on the same `ISIN` and `Date`:

```text
overlap_ns(i,j) = max(0, min(end_i,end_j) - max(start_i,start_j))
overlap_fraction(i,j) = overlap_ns(i,j) / duration_ns(i)
```

For zero-duration targets, the point-in-time rule is used:

```text
start_j <= start_i <= end_j  =>  overlap_fraction(i,j) = 1
```

The key active-overlap features are:

- `overlap_count_tw_all`: time-weighted number of other active metaorders
- `overlap_gross_q_tw_over_Q_all`: active overlapping volume divided by target `Q`
- `overlap_active_imbalance_tw_all`: target-direction signed active imbalance
- `overlap_net_signed_q_tw_over_Q_all`: signed active overlap divided by target `Q`
- `*_prop_env` and `*_client_env`: the same features restricted to proprietary
  or client environment metaorders

## 1. Crowding Raises Impact, but More for Clients in the Tercile Test

The first test sorts proprietary and client metaorders separately into terciles
of daily aligned crowding `c_i`, then refits the standard impact curve inside
each tercile:

```text
I = Y * phi^gamma,    phi = Q / V
```

![Impact curves by crowding tercile](../images/ftsemib/crowding_impact/png/main_crowding_impact_curves.png)

Interactive version:
[`main_crowding_impact_curves.html`](../images/ftsemib/crowding_impact/html/main_crowding_impact_curves.html)

The high-minus-low crowding premium is positive for both groups:

| Group | `phi = 10^-3` | 95% CI | `phi = 10^-2` | 95% CI |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Client | `0.004958` | `[0.004484, 0.005794]` | `0.011348` | `[0.009676, 0.012846]` |
| Proprietary | `0.001860` | `[0.001552, 0.002132]` | `0.005920` | `[0.005022, 0.006845]` |

The group difference goes in the opposite direction from a simple proprietary
crowding explanation:

| Benchmark size | Prop high-low minus client high-low | 95% CI |
|---|---:|---:|
| `phi = 10^-3` | `-0.003098` | `[-0.003770, -0.002715]` |
| `phi = 10^-2` | `-0.005428` | `[-0.006443, -0.004236]` |

Interpretation:

- high-crowding metaorders have higher fitted impact;
- this is true for both proprietary and client flow;
- the unconditional crowding premium is larger for clients;
- therefore this test does **not** explain the proprietary impact premium.

When the same tercile exercise is repeated within broad participation-rate

## 3. Cell-Level Regressions: Crowding Matters, but the Proprietary Dummy Remains

The joint-bin regression controls for participation rate, realized volume share,
and absolute imbalance:

```text
log(E[I | cell]) =
    alpha + beta_eta log(eta)
          + beta_v log(V_t/V)
          + beta_c abs(imb)
          + beta_p 1{proprietary}
```

Cells are built using common pooled bins in `(eta, V_t/V, abs(imb))`. The retained
fit uses `111` cells out of `176` constructed cells.

![Pooled log regression coefficients](../images/ftsemib/crowding_impact/png/pooled_log_regression_coefficients.png)

Interactive version:
[`pooled_log_regression_coefficients.html`](../images/ftsemib/crowding_impact/html/pooled_log_regression_coefficients.html)

Pooled regression coefficients:

| Term | Estimate | Analytic SE | 95% CI | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| `log(eta)` | `0.2274` | `0.0074` | `[0.2127, 0.2420]` | Higher participation raises impact. |
| `log(V_t/V)` | `0.2653` | `0.0058` | `[0.2539, 0.2767]` | Larger realized volume share raises impact. |
| `abs(imb)` | `0.0837` | `0.0443` | `[-0.0042, 0.1716]` | Positive but borderline. |
| `1{proprietary}` | `0.1734` | `0.0197` | `[0.1343, 0.2124]` | Proprietary premium remains. |

The proprietary dummy implies approximately:

```text
exp(0.1734) - 1 = 18.9%
```

higher mean impact at comparable cell-level `eta`, `V_t/V`, and `abs(imb)`.

Separate group regressions give:

| Group | `beta_c` on `abs(imb)` | Bootstrap CI |
|---|---:|---:|
| Client | `0.0845` | approximately includes zero |
| Proprietary | `0.0768` | positive and significant in the current run |
| Prop minus client | `-0.0078` | `[-0.1920, 0.1249]` |

Interpretation:

- residual crowding is positively related to impact;
- the proprietary dummy is still large after these controls;
- the `abs(imb)` slope is not statistically larger for proprietary flow;
- this supports "crowding contributes" but rejects "crowding fully explains".

## 4. Start-Bin Intraday Crowding Is Useful but Not the Final Mechanism

The intraday start-bin analysis asks whether metaorders starting in the same
10-minute bin are aligned with the local same-bin environment. It is useful for
understanding the time-of-day structure of crowding, but it is not the main
causal proxy because it does not track true execution overlap.

![Intraday start-bin crowding profile](../images/ftsemib/crowding_intraday_profile/png/crowding_intraday_profile_10min.png)

Interactive version:
[`crowding_intraday_profile_10min.html`](../images/ftsemib/crowding_intraday_profile/html/crowding_intraday_profile_10min.html)

The main descriptive message is that crowding is not constant over the trading
day. It is strongest in specific intraday windows and weaker near the close. This
motivated the stricter active-overlap analysis below.

## 5. Active Overlap: The Key New Result

The active-overlap analysis directly addresses the mechanical hypothesis:

> Since proprietary metaorders are more numerous, are they more often overlapped
> by other active metaorders on the same stock-day, and does that explain their
> higher impact?

The answer is mostly no for the first part, and partly yes for the second.

![Active overlap feature distributions](../images/ftsemib/crowding_overlap_analysis/png/overlap_feature_distributions.png)

Interactive version:
[`overlap_feature_distributions.html`](../images/ftsemib/crowding_overlap_analysis/html/overlap_feature_distributions.html)

### 5.1 Proprietary Metaorders Are Not More Overlapped in Count

`overlap_count_tw_all` is the average number of other active metaorders during
the life of the target, weighted by the target's execution time.

| Group | Mean | Median | P25 | P75 | P95 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Client | `5.57` | `5.40` | `3.67` | `7.28` | `10.07` |
| Proprietary | `5.36` | `5.15` | `3.41` | `7.06` | `9.95` |

Client targets actually have slightly higher time-weighted active counts.
Therefore, the evidence does not support the statement:

> Proprietary impact is higher because proprietary metaorders are more often
> overlapped by other active metaorders.

### 5.2 Gross Active Volume Is Also Not a Proprietary-Specific Explanation

`overlap_gross_q_tw_over_Q_all` measures how much active overlapping volume the
target faces, normalized by the target's own size.

| Group | Mean | Median | P25 | P75 | P95 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Client | `36.66` | `10.90` | `4.06` | `28.48` | `119.91` |
| Proprietary | `25.55` | `11.14` | `4.54` | `26.40` | `88.76` |

The medians are almost identical. The client mean is higher because of a heavier
right tail: some client targets are small relative to the active environment.

### 5.3 Directional Active Imbalance Is the Main Difference

`overlap_active_imbalance_tw_all` measures whether the active overlapping volume
is aligned with the target direction.

| Group | Mean | Median | P25 | P75 | P95 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Client | `0.0002` | `0.0014` | `-0.5063` | `0.5080` | `0.9193` |
| Proprietary | `0.0261` | `0.0382` | `-0.5129` | `0.5744` | `0.9831` |

This is the first overlap statistic that clearly separates the groups in the
expected direction. Proprietary targets are not more overlapped in count, but
their overlapping flow is more often aligned with their own direction.

Same-sign and opposite-sign active volumes tell the same story:

| Group | Same-sign active volume | Opposite-sign active volume |
|---|---:|---:|
| Client | `177,728` | `183,110` |
| Proprietary | `172,275` | `153,453` |

Client overlap is roughly balanced or slightly opposite. Proprietary overlap is
more same-direction than opposite-direction.

### 5.5 Impact Regressions with Active Overlap

The active-overlap regression estimates:

```text
M0: Impact ~ proprietary

M1: Impact ~ proprietary
             + log(Q/V) + log(Participation Rate)
             + log(duration_minutes)
             + C(start_bin_id) + C(ISIN) + C(Date)

M2: M1 + log1p(overlap_count_tw_all)
          + log1p(overlap_gross_q_tw_over_Q_all)
          + overlap_net_signed_q_tw_over_Q_all
          + overlap_active_imbalance_tw_all
```

`statsmodels` OLS estimate with Date-cluster standard errors, not the sparse
fallback estimator. The regression output is in:

- active count matters modestly;

### 5.6 Power-law-style Binned WLS with Active Overlap

The metaorder-level regressions above are useful because they keep individual
observations and allow Date-cluster standard errors. However, the original
evidence for the proprietary impact premium came from the repository's standard
power-law fit: log-binned `Q/V` cells, `log(mean Impact)` as the dependent
variable, and WLS weights based on the delta-method variance of
`log(mean Impact)`.

To align the active-overlap test with that original evidence, the script now
also estimates:

```text
W0: log(mean Impact_cell) ~ proprietary + log(center Q/V bin)

W1: W0 + log(mean Participation Rate_cell)
        + log(mean duration_minutes_cell)

W2: W1 + log(mean overlap_count_tw_all_cell)
        + log(mean same-sign overlap/Q_cell)
        + log(mean opposite-sign overlap/Q_cell)
```

The cell is always the same object as in the standard impact curves:
`group x Q/V-log-bin`. The `Q/V` regressor is the geometric center of the
log-bin, exactly as in `metaorder_computation.py`. Additional controls summarize
the composition of that same cell and are averaged before taking logs. The
minimum retained-cell size is 20 metaorders, the same `MIN_COUNT` used by the
standard impact fit. Signed overlap variables are not included linearly in this
WLS specification; directional crowding is represented through the same-sign and
opposite-sign positive components.
The output is in:

[`overlap_impact_wls_regressions.csv`](../out_files/ftsemib/crowding_overlap_analysis/overlap_impact_wls_regressions.csv)

![Proprietary coefficient across binned WLS active-overlap regressions](../images/ftsemib/crowding_overlap_analysis/png/overlap_impact_wls_proprietary_coefficient.png)

Interactive version:
[`overlap_impact_wls_proprietary_coefficient.html`](../images/ftsemib/crowding_overlap_analysis/html/overlap_impact_wls_proprietary_coefficient.html)

The proprietary coefficient is now a log-impact coefficient, so
`exp(coef)-1` is the approximate multiplicative premium.

| Model | Proprietary log coefficient | Approx. premium | SE | 95% CI | Cells | Metaorders |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| W0 | `0.1793` | `19.6%` | `0.0335` | `[0.1137, 0.2450]` | `51` | `833,795` |
| W1 | `0.1821` | `20.0%` | `0.0563` | `[0.0716, 0.2925]` | `51` | `833,795` |
| W2 | `0.1397` | `15.0%` | `0.1044` | `[-0.0650, 0.3445]` | `51` | `833,795` |

This is the closest WLS analogue of the impact-curve evidence. W1 shows that
cell-level participation rate and duration do not absorb the proprietary
premium. W2 adds active-overlap summaries on exactly the same 51 curve cells;
the proprietary coefficient falls from `0.1793`/`0.1821` to `0.1397` and becomes
statistically indistinguishable from zero.

The result should still be presented cautiously, but not dismissed as an
irrelevant coarse exercise: this binned WLS is the same empirical language used
by the impact-curve literature. Its limitation is that only 51 cell means enter
the regression, so the metaorder-level OLS remains the richer robustness check.
The robust conclusion is therefore not that overlap fully explains everything in
every specification, but that active directional overlap is the crowding measure
that materially weakens the proprietary premium in the same framework where the
premium was first observed.

## 6. Synthesis for Section 5.4

The results now support a layered answer.

### What crowding explains

Crowding explains part of the cross-sectional variation in impact:

- high-crowding terciles have higher fitted impact;
- `eta`, realized volume share, and `abs(imb)` jointly explain a large part of
  cell-level mean impact;
- active directional overlap has a strong positive coefficient in metaorder-level
  regressions;
- adding active-overlap features reduces the metaorder-level proprietary
  coefficient by `15.7%`;
- in the power-law-style binned WLS, adding active-overlap controls reduces the
  positive proprietary log-premium and makes it statistically insignificant.

### What crowding does not explain

Crowding does not fully explain the proprietary premium:

- the unconditional high-minus-low crowding premium is larger for clients;
- proprietary targets do not have more time-weighted active overlap;
- gross overlapping volume normalized by target size is not higher for
  proprietary targets;
- the proprietary dummy remains positive after controls in the richer
  metaorder-level active-overlap regression;
- the binned WLS result is deliberately aligned with the impact-curve evidence,
  but it is still based on a small number of curve cells.

### Most defensible paper statement

A concise statement for the paper would be:

> Crowding is economically relevant: metaorders executed in more crowded
> environments exhibit larger impact, and active directional overlap is strongly
> associated with impact at the metaorder level. However, the larger impact of
> proprietary metaorders is not a purely mechanical consequence of proprietary
> flow being more numerous or more overlapped. Proprietary targets face similar,
> and in some metrics lower, time-weighted active overlap than client targets.
> What distinguishes them is that their overlapping flow is more directionally
> aligned. Active-overlap controls reduce the proprietary coefficient by about
> 16% in metaorder-level regressions. In the binned log-WLS specification that
> mirrors the original power-law impact fit on `Q/V` cells, active-overlap
> controls reduce the proprietary coefficient and make it statistically
> insignificant. This estimate should be read cautiously because it is based on
> a small number of curve cells, but that is also the empirical object used by
> the impact-curve literature.

## 7. Open Points Before Freezing the Paper Version

The current evidence is strong enough for a subsection draft, but the paper
version should be careful about the following points.

1. The M2 active-overlap regression loses about `4,246` observations because
   some overlap controls are undefined, mainly when active gross overlap is zero.
2. The W2 binned WLS regression is based on `51` retained curve cells; it should
   be reported as the power-law-style counterpart, not as a replacement for the
   metaorder-level regression with fixed effects.
3. The daily crowding and active-overlap measures answer related but distinct
   questions: stock-day alignment versus true interval overlap.
4. The causal interpretation should remain cautious. The analyses show that
   active directional crowding is associated with impact and partially mediates
   the proprietary premium; they do not prove that overlap causes the premium.
