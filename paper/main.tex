\begin{abstract}
Understanding how large orders move prices is central to execution-cost prediction and to models
of price formation. While the average impact of metaorders is known to be concave in size, less
is known about how impact and crowding differ across trading capacities. Using a
trade-by-trade dataset for FTSEMIB constituents (June 2024--May 2025) in which aggressive trades
are attributed to members and labelled as proprietary or client, we reconstruct member-level
metaorders as same-sign, same-client sequences within a day. We normalise size by daily volume and
impact by a realised-kernel volatility proxy, estimate log-binned impact laws, and measure
crowding through volume-weighted order-flow imbalances with day-cluster bootstrap uncertainty. Our
results confirm a robust concave impact relation in both groups, with client flow broadly
consistent with the square-root benchmark and proprietary flow displaying a more concave scaling.
The time-resolved impact profiles further indicate that proprietary impact decays more slowly
after execution, suggesting a larger persistent component. Crowding is economically meaningful in
both segments and strengthens with execution aggressiveness, while cross-group patterns are
consistent with partial intermediation between client demand and proprietary activity.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A robust empirical regularity in market microstructure is the
non-linear, concave relation between the average price impact of large
orders and their size. When a metaorder of total size $Q$ is executed
over a day by slicing it into many smaller trades, the average price
change $\Delta p$ typically scales as a power law,
\begin{equation}
  \Delta p \propto Q^\gamma, \qquad 0 < \gamma < 1,
\end{equation}
with a ``square-root law'' $\gamma \approx \tfrac12$ often observed
across markets and asset classes \cite{BouchaudFarmerLillo2008,SatoKanazawa2024}.
This behaviour has been documented in a wide range of institutional
datasets and synthetic metaorder reconstructions, and is remarkably
robust to variations in the microstructure and in the autocorrelation
of order flow.

At the same time, institutional trading activity is highly crowded:
different investors often trade in the same direction on the same names
and days, which amplifies execution costs throug co-impact.
In co-impact models, the aggregate price response is driven by the joint
distribution of metaorder signs and sizes, with correlations in order
flow leading to a non-linear impact surface and to important differences
between idiosyncratic and crowded trades \cite{BucciEtAl2020}.

The present analysis focuses on a high-frequency dataset where
trade-by-trade aggressive activity can be attributed to individual
members and separated into proprietary and client flows. We:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item reconstruct metaorders as same-sign sequences of aggressive
        trades at the member level;
  \item document the empirical distributions of their durations, sizes,
        relative sizes in units of daily volume, and execution
        participation rates;
  \item estimate a volatility-normalised power-law impact function as a
        function of the metaorder's size;
  \item study crowding for proprietary and client flows via volume-weighted imbalances
        within-group (e.g. proprietary vs proprietary), cross-group (e.g. proprietary vs client), and all-vs-all 
        (e.g. proprietary + client vs proprietary + client)
        ;
  % \item relate the empirical crowding patterns to co-impact and
  %       transaction-cost modelling frameworks where partially observable
  %       order-flow imbalance plays a key role \cite{BriereEtAl2019,Guillaume2025}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Metaorder definition at member level}
Before detailing how we construct metaorders from our dataset, it is useful to briefly review the main approaches that 
have been proposed in the literature to identify such metaorders. Our dataset is particularly valuable in this respect, 
as it provides access to both client and member identifiers as well as detailed information on the nature of each trade. 
These features substantially improve the accuracy of our metaorder reconstruction, placing it just below datasets in which 
metaorders are explicitly labelled, such as in \cite{zarinelli}. In summary, the literature distinguishes four broad 
categories of metaorder detection methods:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Labelled metaorders
    \cite{zarinelli,BucciEtAl2018,BucciEtAl2020Invariants}

    \item Synthetic metaorders construction
    \cite{MaitrierEtAl2025}

    \item Detection of same sign sequences
    \cite{MoroEtAl2009,VaglicaEtAl2010}

    \item Detection of same sign sequences from proprietary data with label on the nature of the trades (proprietary and client) 
    \cite{GomesWaelbroeck2015}
\end{itemize}


\paragraph{Metaorder construction} We work at the member level: a metaorder is a sequence of
trades routed by a given member, for a given client in a given ISIN and day, with constant sign of
aggressive activity. The construction proceeds as follows.

Fix an instrument $k$ and a member $a$. Let $I_{a,k}$ be the ordered set
of indices $j$ such that member $a$ trades instrument $k$ at time
$t_j \in [09{:}30,17{:}30]$. For each $j \in I_{a,k}$ we observe the
sign $\varepsilon_j$ and volume $q_j$.

We first detect runs of constant sign:
\begin{equation}
  M = \{j_s, j_s+1, \dots, j_e\} \subset I_{a,k}
\end{equation}
such that
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon_j = \varepsilon_{j_s}, \qquad \forall j \in M,
\end{equation}
and $M$ is maximal with this property (no strictly larger contiguous
set has a constant sign). We apply several filters to the detected sequences:

\begin{itemize}
\item  A sequence is kept if $|M|$ $\geq$ 5
  \item All trades in the run must belong to the same
        calendar day; runs spanning the market close are discarded.
  \item All trades in the run share the same
        client identifier; this ensures that we do not aggregate orders
        from different final investors.
\end{itemize}

Even after this step, very long runs can contain long periods of
inactivity. We finally split runs on large gaps. Let $\Delta t_k = t_{j_{k+1}} - t_{j_k}$ be
successive inter-trade gaps within a run. If
\begin{equation}
  \Delta t_k > 1 \text{ hour},
\end{equation}
the run is split at that point into separate metaorders. Again, 
metaorders with fewer than five trades are discarded. This yields a collection of same-day, same-client, same-sign metaorders for each ISIN.

Each resulting metaorder $i$ is characterised by:

\begin{itemize}
  \item its set of child trades $M_i = \{j_s(i),\dots,j_e(i)\}$,
  \item its sign $\varepsilon_i \in \{+1,-1\}$ (direction), taken as the sign of
        its trades,
  \item its start and end timestamps $t_i^{\text{start}} = t_{j_s(i)}$
        and $t_i^{\text{end}} = t_{j_e(i)}$,
  \item its execution interval $[t_i^{\text{start}},t_i^{\text{end}}]$,
  \item the capacity flag (proprietary vs client (non-proprietary)).
\end{itemize}

We thus obtain two large metaorder datasets:

\begin{itemize}
  \item proprietary metaorders (group ``prop''),
  \item client (non-proprietary) metaorders (group ``client'') \footnote{It is crucial to emphasize that a portion of 
  trades labeled as client trades are in fact institutional executions carried out by entities with direct market access 
  on behalf of end-clients who lack such access. These are not retail trades.}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Per-metaorder quantities}

For each metaorder $i$ we compute:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Metaorder volume
        \begin{equation}
          Q_i = \sum_{j\in M_i} q_j,
        \end{equation}
        i.e.\ the total number of shares executed by the metaorder.

  \item Metaorder direction.
        \begin{equation}
            \varepsilon_i = \begin{cases}
                +1 \quad \text{buy metaorder} \\ -1 \quad \text{sell metaorder}
            \end{cases}
        \end{equation}
  \item Duration
        \begin{equation}
          T_i = t_i^{\text{end}} - t_i^{\text{start}}.
        \end{equation}

  \item Inter-arrival times. Within each metaorder, gaps between
        consecutive child trades are
        \begin{equation}
          \Delta t_i^{(\ell)} = t_{j_{\ell+1}} - t_{j_\ell}, \qquad \ell = 1,\dots, |M_i|-1.
        \end{equation}

\item Average Daily volume.
      In order to smooth day–to–day fluctuations, we consider
      the 5‑day average daily volume
      \begin{equation}
        \bar V^{(5)}_d = \frac{1}{K_d} \sum_{k=0}^{K_d-1} V_{d-k},
      \end{equation}
      where $K_d = \min\bigl(5,\ \#\{\text{trading days up to } d\}\bigr)$ and
       \begin{equation}
        V_d = \sum_{j \in J_d} q_j,
      \end{equation}
      where $J_d$ are all trades in that ISIN on day $d$.


  \item Daily volatility. For each day, we compute the intraday volatility using a realised-kernel estimator. See Section \ref{subsec:daily_vol}

  \item Relative size (participation in daily volume).
        \begin{equation}
          \phi_i = \frac{Q_i}{V_{d(i)}} \in (0,1],
        \end{equation}
        where $d(i)$ denotes the day of metaorder $i$. This is the
        fraction of the daily traded volume executed by the metaorder.

  \item Participation rate within the execution window. Let
        $V_i^{\text{window}}$ denote the total market volume (buy + sell)
        traded in the same instrument between $t_i^{\text{start}}$ and
        $t_i^{\text{end}}$. The execution participation rate is
        \begin{equation}
          \eta_i = \frac{Q_i}{V_i^{\text{window}}}.
        \end{equation}
        This is the metaorder's market share during its own execution.

  \item Start and end prices. $P_i^{\text{start}}$ and
        $P_i^{\text{end}}$, typically taken as last-traded prices at the
        corresponding timestamps.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Volatility signature plots}

To construct a robust daily volatility for each instrument, we study
volatility signature plots. For a given sampling interval $\Delta$ (in
seconds), the last-traded price is resampled at this frequency within
the trading day, and log-returns are computed. For each day and $\Delta$
we compute realised variance (RV), bipower variation (BPV), and realised
kernel (RK). For each $\Delta$, the daily estimates are aggregated
across days to produce means and standard errors.

An example signature plot for a given ISIN and the client
subset is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:signature}.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{images/member_non_proprietary/png/signature_plots/signature_plot_IT0000062072.png}
  \caption{Example volatility signature plot (client flow for a
  given ISIN). The three panels display mean $\pm 2\text{SE}$ of realised
  variance, bipower variation, and realised kernel as a function of the
  sampling interval $\Delta$ (colors: RV = blue, BPV = green, RK = red).}
  \label{fig:signature}

Realised kernel (RK) with $\Delta = 1000$ seconds is used as the daily
volatility proxy in the impact analysis below. The value has been chosen looking at the signature plot evaluated using 
the RK estimation, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:signature}. This differs from approaches that rely on simpler 
open-to-close or range-based daily volatility proxies.

\section{Normalisation and Impact Model}
\label{sec:impact_model}

To compare metaorders across instruments and dates, we normalise sizes by daily volume and impacts by a daily volatility 
proxy. Building on these normalised quantities, we estimate an average impact law as a function of relative size and 
study how it varies between proprietary and client flow.

\subsection{Daily volatility and size normalisation}
\label{subsec:daily_vol}

For each instrument and day $d$, we construct a daily volatility
$\sigma_d$ as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Restrict the trade-time price process $P_t$ to trades on day
        $d$, and retain the last price at each timestamp.

  \item For each trading day, we build an equally spaced time grid with step
$\Delta = 1000$ seconds. At each grid time $t_\ell$ we set $P_{t_\ell}$
equal to the last observed trade price strictly before (or at) $t_\ell$
(previous-tick interpolation), yielding a sequence
$P_{t_0}, P_{t_1}, \dots, P_{t_n}$ with $t_{\ell+1} - t_\ell = \Delta$.

  \item Compute log-returns
        \begin{equation}
          r_\ell = \log P_{t_{\ell+1}} - \log P_{t_\ell}, \qquad \ell=0,\dots,n-1.
        \end{equation}

  \item Apply a realised-kernel estimator with a Parzen kernel and
        bandwidth $H \propto n^{2/3}$ to $(r_\ell)_{\ell=0}^{n-1}$ to
        obtain $\sigma_d = \text{RK}_d$.
\end{enumerate}

Size is normalised by daily volume $V_d$, as defined in
Section~\ref{sec:data_metaorders}. For metaorder $i$ on day $d(i)$ we
use
\begin{equation}
  \phi_i = \frac{Q_i}{V_{d(i)}}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Instantaneous impact in volatility units}

For each metaorder $i$, let $P_i^{\text{start}}$ and
$P_i^{\text{end}}$ be the prices at the first and last trade of the
metaorder. The signed log-return over the execution interval is
\begin{equation}
  \Delta p_i = \log P_i^{\text{end}} - \log P_i^{\text{start}}.
\end{equation}
Let $\varepsilon_i \in \{+1,-1\}$ be the direction (buy $+1$, sell $-1$), and
$\sigma_{d(i)}$ the daily volatility on the same day. The
instantaneous impact of metaorder $i$ in volatility units is
defined as
\begin{equation}
  I_i = \frac{\varepsilon_i\,\Delta p_i}{\sigma_{d(i)}}.
\end{equation}
By construction, $I_i$ is typically positive on average (buys push the
price up, sells push it down), but individual realisations can be
negative because of noise and concurrent market activity.

Before fitting, we apply a minimum-size filter $\phi_i > 10^{-5}$ to avoid the extremely noisy region. In the WLS step 
we also exclude the few metaorders with participation rate equal to one by imposing $\eta_i < 1$.

This yields a clean dataset of $(\phi_i, I_i)$ pairs with auxiliary
fields (participation, capacity, etc.) for subsequent analysis.

\subsection{Impact models}
\label{subsec:impact_models}
Following \cite{zarinelli}, we posit two different functional forms for the relation between the expected 
instantaneous impact and the relative size:
\begin{align}
  \E[I_i \mid \phi_i] &= Y\,\phi_i^\gamma \qquad &\text{Power law} \nonumber \\
  \E[I_i \mid \phi_i] &= a \log_{10}(1+b\phi_i) \qquad &\text{Logarithm}
  \label{eq:impact_model}
\end{align}
where:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $Y > 0$ is a scale parameter (impact for unit participation);
  \item $\gamma \in (0,1)$ is the impact exponent (with $\gamma \approx \tfrac12$ corresponding to a square-root law 
  \cite{BouchaudFarmerLillo2008,SatoKanazawa2024});
  \item $a, b > 0$ are the parameters of the logarithmic model, which serves as a benchmark to assess deviations 
  from power-law behaviour (e.g. saturation or linear regimes).
\end{itemize}

Taking logarithms of the power-law specification yields:
\begin{equation}
  \log \E[I_i \mid \phi_i] = \log Y + \gamma \log \phi_i.
\end{equation}

Because individual $I_i$ are noisy and often negative, we do not regress directly on single metaorders. 
Instead we proceed via log-binning.

\paragraph{Binning in relative size.}

We form $K$ logarithmic bins in $\log_{10} \phi$ over the range of the data. For each bin $k$ we compute:

\begin{itemize}
  \item the bin centre $\phi_k$ (e.g.\ geometric mean of $\phi_i$ in the bin);
  \item the mean impact $\bar{I}_k$ over metaorders in the bin;
  \item the standard error of the mean $\text{SEM}_k$.
\end{itemize}

Bins with fewer than a prescribed minimum count, non-positive or non-finite $\bar{I}_k$, 
or non-finite $\text{SEM}_k$ are discarded.

\paragraph{Estimation procedure: Power Law.}

For the power-law model, we define
\begin{equation}
  X_k = \log \phi_k, \qquad Z_k = \log \bar{I}_k.
\end{equation}
We fit the linear model
\begin{equation}
  Z_k = \alpha + \gamma X_k + \varepsilon_k,
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = \log Y$ and $\gamma$ is the impact exponent. The fit is performed by weighted least squares (WLS) 
in log-space, using weights inversely proportional to the variance of the bin estimate \cite{BouchaudFarmerLillo2008}:
\begin{equation}
    w_k \approx \left(\frac{\bar{I}_k}{\text{SEM}_k}\right)^2.
\end{equation}
Bins with more metaorders and smaller dispersion are given larger weight, while very noisy or poorly populated bins 
contribute less. This yields estimates $\hat{\alpha}$ and $\hat{\gamma}$ together with their standard errors; the 
scale parameter is then recovered as $Y = e^{\hat{\alpha}}$.

\paragraph{Estimation procedure: Logarithmic Benchmark.}

Alongside the power law, we fit the logarithmic specification $\bar{I}_k = a \log_{10}(1+b\phi_k)$ on the same 
binned statistics $(\phi_k, \bar{I}_k)$. Unlike the power law, this model is estimated via a non-linear fit on the 
bin means. Comparing the two curves allows us to visually inspect deviations from the power law, particularly at the 
extremes of the size distribution where the logarithmic form imposes a linear behaviour for $\phi \to 0$ and a slower 
saturation for very large $\phi$.

\paragraph{Empirical impact curves.}

Applying this procedure separately to proprietary and client metaorders yields the impact curves in 
Figure~\ref{fig:impact_vs_size}. After the $Q/V > 10^{-5}$ filter and the $\eta<1$ filter, we retain 
$588172$ proprietary and $255499$ client metaorders. The WLS estimates are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item proprietary: $Y = 0.07264 \pm 0.00366$, $\gamma = 0.3662 \pm 0.0083$;
  \item client (non-proprietary): $Y = 0.1281 \pm 0.0154$, $\gamma = 0.5082 \pm 0.0228$.
\end{itemize}
The client exponent is close to the square-root benchmark, while proprietary flow displays a markedly more concave 
scaling. Moreover, the logarithmic benchmark provides an excellent linear-scale fit for client flow (in the current run, 
$R^2_{\text{lin}}\approx 0.993$), while for proprietary flow the power law remains the better description in both log 
and linear scales.

Splitting by side yields very similar parameters for proprietary buys and sells, while client flow shows a clearer

\subsection{Execution and aftermath impact paths}
\label{subsec:impact-paths}

The scalar impact measure $I_i$ in the previous section captures only the
price change between the first and last trade of a metaorder. To obtain a time--resolved view of
how impact builds up during execution and relaxes afterwards, we also track the full impact path of
each metaorder.

For a given metaorder $i$ on day $d(i)$, with start and end times $t^{\mathrm{start}}_i$ and
$t^{\mathrm{end}}_i$ and duration $T_i = t^{\mathrm{end}}_i - t^{\mathrm{start}}_i$, we define the
instantaneous normalised impact at calendar time $t \ge t^{\mathrm{start}}_i$ as
\begin{equation}
  I_i(t) = \frac{\varepsilon_i}{\sigma_{d(i)}}\bigl(\log P(t) - \log P^{\mathrm{start}}_i\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon_i \in \{+1,-1\}$ is the metaorder direction and $\sigma_{d(i)}$ is the daily volatility
constructed in Section~\ref{subsec:daily_vol}. This is the same normalisation used for
the end-of-execution impact, but evaluated along the whole trajectory of the price process.

We then introduce a normalised time variable $\tau$ at the metaorder level. During execution,
\begin{equation}
  \tau = \frac{t - t^{\mathrm{start}}_i}{T_i} \in [0,1] \qquad
  (t^{\mathrm{start}}_i \le t \le t^{\mathrm{end}}_i),
\end{equation}
so that $\tau = 0$ corresponds to the first trade and $\tau = 1$ to the last trade. In the
aftermath, we continue to track impact on a time window proportional to the execution duration,
\begin{equation}
  \tau = 1 + \frac{t - t^{\mathrm{end}}_i}{T_i} \in (1, 1 + \lambda],
\end{equation}
for $t^{\mathrm{end}}_i < t \le t^{\mathrm{end}}_i + \lambda T_i$, where $\lambda > 0$ is a fixed
multiple (in practice, $\lambda \approx 2$). For each metaorder we record:
\begin{itemize}
  \item a partial impact path, i.e.\ the sequence of $I_i(t)$ evaluated after each child
        trade during execution ($\tau \in [0,1]$);
  \item an aftermath impact path, i.e.\ $I_i(t)$ sampled at a set of evenly spaced times in
        the interval $(1,1+\lambda]$.
\end{itemize}
These two vectors are stored per metaorder and subsequently interpolated onto a common normalised time grid 
$\{\tau_m\}_{m=0}^M \subset [0,1+\lambda]$. Averaging across metaorders in a given group $G$ (proprietary or client) 
yields the mean normalised impact path
\begin{equation}
  \bar I_G(\tau_m) = \frac{1}{N_G(\tau_m)} \sum_{i \in G(\tau_m)} I_i(\tau_m),
\end{equation}
where $G(\tau_m)$ is the set of metaorders in group $G$ with a defined impact at time $\tau_m$,
and $N_G(\tau_m) = |G(\tau_m)|$. The sample used here is the same filtered metaorder set as in the
power-law fits (minimum duration, minimum relative size, finite impact).

Figure~\ref{fig:normalized_impact_paths} displays the mean paths for proprietary and client (non-proprietary) metaorders, 
both overall and split by buy/sell. The vertical dashed line marks $\tau = 1$, the end of execution; $\tau > 1$ corresponds 
to the aftermath window.

For client metaorders, the mean impact increases smoothly over the execution interval and peaks at $\tau \approx 1$. Over 
the aftermath window (up to $\tau=3$ in our baseline export), the impact relaxes to $\bar I(\tau{=}3)/\bar I(\tau{=}1)\approx 0.57$, 
indicating a sizeable transient component.
The buy/sell split highlights a pronounced asymmetry for client flow: sell metaorders retain more impact (about 0.67) than 
buys (about 0.47) at $\tau=3$ relative to $\tau=1$; proprietary buy/sell paths are instead very similar (retention about 0.74--0.76).

For proprietary metaorders, the decay is slower and more incomplete, with $\bar I(\tau{=}3)/\bar I(\tau{=}1)\approx 0.75$. 
This suggests that proprietary metaorders leave a more persistent footprint in prices, whereas client metaorders are associated 
with a larger transient component that tends to mean-revert. In line with the literature, the persistent component is often 
interpreted as a proxy for the information content of the metaorder \cite{GomesWaelbroeck2015}.
Overall, the dynamic profiles are in line with the picture of transient but partly permanent impact emerging from 
empirical studies of metaorders and propagator models: impact accumulates during execution, then relaxes slowly towards 
a non-zero asymptotic level rather than reverting to
zero.
% \footnote{See, for example, the discussion of impact kernels and slow decay in
% Bouchaud, Farmer, and Lillo~\cite{bouchaud2008how}, and the co-impact analysis of
% Bucci et al.~\cite{bucci2020coimpact}, where crowded institutional flows generate persistent price
% moves.} 
In our dataset, the stronger permanence of proprietary impact is consistent with a view in which proprietary trading 
incorporates more information, while client flow is more liquidity-demanding and hence more prone to subsequent partial 
reversal. This dynamic perspective complements the static power-law fits of Section~\ref{subsec:impact_models} and provides 
additional evidence that market impact and its decay depend sensitively on who trades and how their orders are crowded in 
time and across investors.

\section{Crowding and Co-Impact of Proprietary vs Client Flow}
\label{sec:crowding}

We now turn to crowding: the tendency of metaorders to align with
(or against) the contemporaneous net order flow of other metaorders.
Crowding is a key driver of co-impact and execution costs, since
metaorders that trade in the same direction as the rest of the market
face more adverse price pressure. This connects directly to the
institutional co-impact picture of \cite{BucciEtAl2020}.

All crowding measures are based on volume-weighted imbalances
constructed from other metaorders in an appropriate reference set.

\subsection{Within-group crowding}
\label{subsec:local_imbalance}

Fix a group $G$ (proprietary or client). For a given instrument $k$ and
day $d$, let $G_{k,d}$ be the set of metaorders in group $G$ on $(k,d)$.
For each $j \in G_{k,d}$ define the signed volume
\begin{equation}
  Q_j^{\text{sign}} = Q_j \varepsilon_j.
\end{equation}
For metaorder $i \in G_{k,d}$, the orderflow imbalance from
other metaorders in the same stock and day is defined as
\begin{equation}
  \text{imb}_i^{k,d} =
  \frac{\displaystyle \sum_{j \in G_{k,d}\setminus\{i\}} Q_j^{\text{sign}}}
       {\displaystyle \sum_{j \in G_{k,d}\setminus\{i\}} Q_j}.
  \label{eq:local_imbalance}
\end{equation}
Interpretation:

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\text{imb}_i^{k,d} \approx +1$: other metaorders
        that day are almost all buys;
  \item $\text{imb}_i^{k,d} \approx -1$: other metaorders
        are almost all sells;
  \item $\text{imb}_i^{k,d} \approx 0$: buy and sell volume
        roughly balance.
\end{itemize}

If $G_{k,d}$ contains only metaorder $i$ (no ``others''), the
denominator vanishes and $\text{imb}_i^{k,d}$ is set to NaN
and excluded. The distributions of the imbalances within-group and cross-group is shown in 
Figure \ref{fig:imbalance_distributions}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{images/prop_vs_nonprop/png/imbalance_distribution.png}
    \caption{Imbalance distributions: within-group leave-one-out imbalance (top) and cross-group environment imbalance 
    (bottom). Line colors identify group and side as in the legend.}
    \label{fig:imbalance_distributions}
\end{figure}

A simple measure of within-group crowding is the Pearson correlation
between direction and orderflow imbalance,
\begin{equation}
  r = \mathrm{Corr}(\varepsilon_i, \text{imb}_i^{k,d}),
\end{equation}
computed over all metaorders in the group. We also compute daily
correlations $r_d$ by restricting to metaorders on a single date $d$ and
evaluating
\begin{equation}
  r_d = \mathrm{Corr}(\varepsilon_i, \text{imb}_i^{k,d} \mid \text{Date}=d)
\end{equation}
whenever there are at least a minimum number of metaorders on that day (100).
The time series $\{r_d\}$ is plotted for proprietary
and client flows separately, together with rolling averages (e.g. a
3-day rolling mean) to smooth short-term fluctuations, as in
Figure~\ref{fig:daily_crowding_local}.
Metaorders executed on the same trading day are not independent, as they
share market conditions and common shocks. To obtain dependence-aware
uncertainty, we construct confidence intervals via a Date-cluster
bootstrap: we resample trading days with replacement, recompute the
relevant correlation on the resampled panel, and report percentile
$95\%$ confidence intervals (1000 replicates).
On the current dataset, within-group orderflow imbalance statistics are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Proprietary flow.
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Global correlation:
            $r = 0.108$ with $95\%$ CI $[0.102, 0.113]$,
            $n = 588{,}334$ metaorders.
      % \item Conditional means over all days all members and all ISINs:
      %       \[
      %         \E[\mathrm{imb} \mid \varepsilon=+1] \approx -0.024, \quad
      %         \E[\mathrm{imb} \mid \varepsilon=-1] \approx -0.08.
      %       \]
      \item Mean daily correlation:
            $\bar{r}_{\text{prop}} = 0.081$ with $95\%$ CI $[0.077, 0.086]$
            over $251$ days.
    \end{itemize}

  \item Client flow.
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Global correlation:
            $r = 0.187$ with $95\%$ CI $[0.180, 0.196]$,
            $n = 255{,}535$ metaorders.
      % \item Conditional means over all days all members and all ISINs:
      %       \[
      %         \E[\mathrm{imb} \mid \varepsilon=+1] \approx +0.088, \quad
      %         \E[\mathrm{imb} \mid \varepsilon=-1] \approx -0.087.
      %       \]
      \item Mean daily correlation:
            $\bar{r}_{\text{client}} = 0.169$ with $95\%$ CI $[0.162, 0.177]$
            over $251$ days.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

Thus both client and proprietary metaorders tend to trade with the local daily
imbalance (positive crowding/herding), although client metaorders show a higher and statistically

\subsection{Cross-group crowding}
\label{subsec:cross_group_crowding}
To study cross-group crowding, we construct an environment
imbalance based solely on the other group. For example, to ask whether
proprietary metaorders trade with or against the imbalance generated by
clients, we proceed as follows.

Fix a source group $G_{\text{src}}$ (e.g.\ clients) and a target group
$G_{\text{tgt}}$ (e.g.\ proprietary). For each instrument $k$ and day
$d$, let $G_{k,d}^{\text{src}}$ be the set of metaorders in the source
group on $(k,d)$. Define the environment imbalance for that $(k,d)$ as
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{imb}^{\text{env}}_{k,d} =
  \frac{\displaystyle \sum_{j \in G_{k,d}^{\text{src}}} Q_j \varepsilon_j}
       {\displaystyle \sum_{j \in G_{k,d}^{\text{src}}} Q_j}.
\end{equation}
This is then attached to each target-group metaorder $i$ in the same
$(k,d)$ as $\mathrm{imb}^{\text{env}}_i$.

We compute daily correlations
\begin{align}
  r_d^{\text{prop}\mid\text{client}} &=
     \mathrm{Corr}\bigl(\varepsilon_i^{\text{prop}}, \mathrm{imb}^{\text{env}}_i
     \,\big|\, \text{Date}=d\bigr),
     \\
  r_d^{\text{client}\mid\text{prop}} &=
     \mathrm{Corr}\bigl(\varepsilon_i^{\text{client}}, \mathrm{imb}^{\text{env}}_i
     \,\big|\, \text{Date}=d\bigr),
\end{align}
where the environment is built from client or proprietary flow
respectively.

Empirically, over $251$ days:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Global correlations:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item $\mathrm{Corr}(\varepsilon^{\text{prop}}, \mathrm{imb}^{\text{env}}_{\text{client}}) = -0.023$
            with $95\%$ CI $[-0.028, -0.018]$.
      \item $\mathrm{Corr}(\varepsilon^{\text{client}}, \mathrm{imb}^{\text{env}}_{\text{prop}}) = -0.016$
            with $95\%$ CI $[-0.025, -0.007]$.
    \end{itemize}
      \item Mean daily correlations (days with $n \ge 100$):
    \begin{itemize}
      \item $\bar{r}^{\text{prop}\mid\text{client}} = -0.018$ with $95\%$ CI $[-0.023, -0.014]$,
      \item $\bar{r}^{\text{client}\mid\text{prop}} = -0.003$ with $95\%$ CI $[-0.010, 0.004]$.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

The $\text{prop}\mid\text{client}$ daily correlation is slightly negative, indicating a (very) mild tendency for 
proprietary group to trade against the imbalance produced by the client group,
which is reminiscent of the way different institutional styles interact
in \cite{BucciEtAl2020}. The $\text{client}\mid\text{prop}$ correlation is close to zero with a confidence 
interval that includes zero, suggesting no clear alignment or anti-alignment of client metaorders with the 
proprietary environment. The time series of these cross-group correlations is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:daily_crowding_cross}.

\subsection{Crowding conditioned on participation rate}
\label{subsec:crowding_eta}

The execution participation rate $\eta_i = Q_i / V_i^{\text{window}}$ provides a proxy for the
``aggressiveness'' of a metaorder, i.e.\ the fraction of the contemporaneous traded volume
absorbed during its own execution window. We use $\eta_i$ as an execution-state variable and ask
whether crowding becomes stronger at higher participation.

For each group (proprietary and client), we partition metaorders into deciles of $\eta_i$ using
pooled quantiles (common bin edges across groups). Within each bin $b$ we compute two
complementary statistics based on an imbalance proxy $\mathrm{imb}_i$:
\begin{align}
  A_b &= \E[\varepsilon_i\,\mathrm{imb}_i \mid \eta_i \in b],
  \\
  U_b &= \E[|\mathrm{imb}_i| \mid \eta_i \in b].
\end{align}
where $\mathrm{imb}_i$ is either the within-group leave-one-out imbalance $\text{imb}_i^{k,d}$ from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:local_imbalance} or the cross-group environment imbalance $\mathrm{imb}^{\text{env}}_i$
defined above. Confidence bands are obtained via a block bootstrap that resamples trading days with
replacement (1000 replicates), which accounts for cross-sectional dependence within the same date.

\paragraph{Within-group conditioning.}
Figure~\ref{fig:crowding_vs_eta_local} shows that both groups become more crowded as $\eta$
increases. The effect is monotone and much stronger for client flow: high-participation client
metaorders are strongly aligned with the contemporaneous within-client imbalance, while low-$\eta$
metaorders are close to neutral. Proprietary flow exhibits a milder but statistically significant
increase. In addition, $U_b$ increases with $\eta$ for both groups, indicating that high-participation
executions tend to occur on $(k,d)$ pairs where the rest of the same-group flow is more one-sided.

\paragraph{Cross-group conditioning.}
Figure~\ref{fig:crowding_vs_eta_cross} reveals a different pattern across groups. While the magnitude
of the other group's imbalance increases with $\eta$ for both proprietary and client metaorders, the
alignment statistics diverge. Proprietary metaorders are weakly contrarian to the client environment
across $\eta$ with no pronounced trend, whereas client metaorders become increasingly contrarian to
the proprietary environment at high $\eta$. This behaviour is consistent with an
intermediation/risk-transfer picture in which aggressive client demand is partly offset by
proprietary flow.

\subsection{Crowding vs all others}

A third viewpoint concatenates proprietary and client flows, then
computes orderflow imbalances of each metaorder against all other
metaorders (both groups) on the same stock and day:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{imb}^{\text{all}}_i =
  \frac{\displaystyle \sum_{j \in G_{k,d}\setminus\{i\}} Q_j \varepsilon_j}
       {\displaystyle \sum_{j \in G_{k,d}\setminus\{i\}} Q_j},
\end{equation}
where $G_{k,d}$ now contains both proprietary and client metaorders.
The combined dataset is then split back into proprietary and client
subsets, and daily correlations
$\mathrm{Corr}(\varepsilon_i,\mathrm{imb}^{\text{all}}_i)$ are computed for each
group.

Aggregating over days, we obtain mean daily correlations:

\begin{itemize}
  \item proprietary vs all others:
        $\bar{r}^{\text{prop}\mid\text{all}} \approx 0.041$;
  \item client vs all others:
        $\bar{r}^{\text{client}\mid\text{all}} \approx 0.113$.
\end{itemize}

Client flow again shows a stronger alignment with the overall daily
imbalance than proprietary flow, reinforcing the picture that client
metaorders are more crowded.

\subsection{Member level crowding}
The final measure of interest---particularly from a regulatory perspective---is the correlation 
between a member’s proprietary trading direction and the order-flow imbalance generated by the 
clients whose metaorders were executed through that member. This indicator can be interpreted as 
evidence that the member either provides liquidit to its clients’ metaorders (negative correlation) 
or trades in the same direction as the flow, potentially amplifying the market impact of its 
clients’ trades (positive correlation).

For a given member $m$ and date $d$, let $\mathcal{C}_{m,d}$ denote the set of client metaorders executed 
by member $m$ on day $d$, aggregated across all ISINs. We define the member-level client imbalance as
\begin{equation}
\text{imb}_{m,d}
  = \frac{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{C}_{m,d}} Q_j \epsilon_j}
         {\sum_{j \in \mathcal{C}_{m,d}} Q_j},
\end{equation}
where $Q_j$ and $\epsilon_j$ denote, respectively, the size and direction of client metaorder $j$.
The statistic $\text{imb}_{m,d}$ is considered valid only when both the member and its associated 
client cohort initiate a sufficient number of metaorders during day $d$. Using members with at least 30 proprietary and 
30 client metaorders over the sample, there are $10$ such members (out of 45), and the mean per-member correlation 
between proprietary direction and client imbalance is $0.003$.

Aggregating across all member-level observations, the global correlation between proprietary direction and the 
member-level client imbalance is $0.011$ with a bootstrap $95\%$ CI $[0.001, 0.022]$ and a permutation p-value $p=0.014$.

A more informative analysis focuses on how this correlation evolves over time. Periods displaying 
larger absolute values may signal episodes o front running (when $\text{imb}_{m,d} > 0$) 
or systematic liquidity provision (when $\text{imb}_{m,d} < 0$). 
Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap_imbcorr} reports a heatmap of the correlation computed over non-overlapping 
3-day windows. 
As illustrated, most member-window blocks hover near zero, but a handful of windows show stronger 
alignment or opposition (often in small-sample windows), suggesting that prop--client co-movement is 
episodic and member-dependent rather than uniformly persistent.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We set out to ask a simple question: when aggressive trading can be separated into proprietary and client flow, do these two segments
leave the same footprint on prices, and do they become crowded in the same way? To address this, we use trade-by-trade data for
FTSEMIB constituents from 2024/06/03 to 2025/05/30 where each aggressive trade is attributed to a member and labelled by capacity.
We reconstruct member-level metaorders as within-day sequences of same-sign aggressive trades, document their basic execution
properties, and then study impact and crowding on a common scale by normalising size by daily volume and impact by a realised-kernel
volatility proxy. Impact laws are estimated on log-binned averages with weighted least squares, while uncertainty for crowding
statistics is obtained with a day-cluster bootstrap.

Two empirical regularities emerge from the impact analysis. First, average impact is concave in relative size for both segments
(Figure~\ref{fig:impact_vs_size} and Figure~\ref{fig:impact_overlay}), but the scaling differs sharply across capacities. Under our
baseline filters, client flow is close to the square-root benchmark with $\hat\gamma = 0.5082 \pm 0.0228$, whereas proprietary flow
is markedly more concave with $\hat\gamma = 0.3662 \pm 0.0083$ (Section~\ref{subsec:impact_models}). Second, the time-resolved impact
paths show that proprietary impact is more persistent after execution: at $\tau=3$ we find
$\bar I(\tau{=}3)/\bar I(\tau{=}1)\approx0.75$ for proprietary metaorders versus $\approx0.57$ for client metaorders
(Section~\ref{subsec:impact-paths}, Figure~\ref{fig:normalized_impact_paths}). In other words, the concave scaling difference is not
purely static: it is mirrored by a different transient-versus-persistent decomposition of the average response.

Crowding is the second dimension where the two segments diverge. Within-group direction--imbalance correlations are positive and
statistically significant, indicating that both proprietary and client metaorders tend to trade with the contemporaneous imbalance
generated by other same-group metaorders. Quantitatively, the global correlations are $r=0.108$ (95\% CI $[0.102,0.113]$) for
proprietary metaorders and $r=0.187$ (95\% CI $[0.180,0.196]$) for client metaorders, with compatible conclusions from the daily
correlation time series (Section~\ref{subsec:local_imbalance}, Figure~\ref{fig:daily_crowding_local}). Conditioning on participation
rate reveals that crowding strengthens with execution aggressiveness in both groups, and much more steeply for client flow
(Figure~\ref{fig:crowding_vs_eta_local}, Table~\ref{tab:crowding_vs_eta_effects}). Across groups, correlations are small and slightly
negative: proprietary flow is weakly contrarian to the client environment, while high-participation client metaorders become
increasingly contrarian to the proprietary environment (Section~\ref{subsec:cross_group_crowding},
Figure~\ref{fig:daily_crowding_cross}, Figure~\ref{fig:crowding_vs_eta_cross}). Taken together, these patterns are consistent with
partial intermediation and risk transfer between client demand and proprietary activity.

Finally, the member-level diagnostics speak to the question of prop--client co-movement at the level of the executing broker. The
average effect is small (mean per-member correlation $0.003$), but in the pooled sample it is statistically different from zero
(global correlation $0.011$ with 95\% CI $[0.001,0.022]$; permutation $p=0.014$). The heatmap over non-overlapping 3-day windows
(Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap_imbcorr}) suggests that these relationships are episodic and member-dependent, reinforcing the value of
time-localised diagnostics rather than relying only on unconditional averages.
